## 1. What Problem Does This Paper Solve?

The paper addresses two intertwined problems in the audio deepfake detection literature:

**Problem 1 — Incomparable results**: Related work on audio spoofing detection uses wildly inconsistent preprocessing pipelines, feature extraction choices, hyperparameter settings, and fine-tuning procedures. This makes it impossible to determine whether a model's reported success comes from its architecture, its feature extraction, or its preprocessing — the field lacks a controlled comparison.

**Problem 2 — Unknown generalisation to real-world deepfakes**: Every existing audio deepfake detection model is trained and evaluated exclusively on the **ASVspoof dataset**, which is based on VCTK — a corpus of professional studio-recorded speakers in a semi-anechoic chamber. Models may be learning VCTK-specific or ASVspoof-specific artefacts rather than genuine deepfake cues. Whether these models actually work on realistic "in-the-wild" deepfakes (social media clips, political voice fakes) is entirely unknown.

**Specific contributions:**
1. Re-implement 12 architectures from related work under a **uniform evaluation framework** — same training data, same loss, same hyperparameters — to isolate what drives performance
2. Systematically swap feature extraction methods and input lengths across all architectures
3. Create and publish a new **"In-The-Wild" dataset**: 37.9 hours of real and fake audio clips of 58 celebrities and politicians sourced from social media
4. Evaluate all 12 architectures on in-the-wild data to measure real-world generalisation

---

## 2. Why Does This Problem Matter?

- **Audio deepfakes are a growing societal threat**: TTS technology (WaveNet 2016, Tacotron 2017) enables convincing voice cloning of politicians and public figures. Fake audio clips of politicians surrendering, making inflammatory statements, or endorsing products are already circulating on social media
- **The benchmark monopoly creates false confidence**: Reporting near-perfect EER (1–2%) on ASVspoof 2019 LA evaluation creates the impression that audio deepfake detection is largely solved. The paper shows this impression is wrong — models achieving <4% EER on ASVspoof often degrade to >33–70% EER (near-random guessing) on in-the-wild data
- **A 1000% performance degradation is practically catastrophic**: A system that achieves 1.2% EER on ASVspoof achieving 37% EER on real-world data is essentially non-functional. No honest deployment of such a system would be possible
- **Inconsistent benchmarking misleads research investment**: If the community cannot determine whether architecture or feature extraction drives performance, it cannot prioritise the right research directions. Controlled ablation is a prerequisite for scientific progress
- **The real-world threat model differs fundamentally from ASVspoof**: ASVspoof attacks are generated with specific TTS/VC algorithms; in-the-wild deepfakes are generated by diverse, unseen, and rapidly evolving commercial tools. The benchmark distribution does not reflect the deployment distribution

### What Was Missing Before This Paper

- No prior work had evaluated all these architectures under **identical training conditions**
- No prior work had measured performance on **out-of-domain, in-the-wild** audio deepfakes
- The choice of melspec vs. logspec vs. cqtspec had never been systematically ablated across all architectures
- The effect of fixed-length (4s) vs. variable-length input had never been isolated as an independent variable
- The companion paper (Müller et al., 2106.12914, "Speech is Silver, Silence is Golden") suggested that ASVspoof-trained models may be learning **silence patterns** rather than deepfake artefacts — a prior signal of potential generalisation failure

## 4. What Does This Paper Propose?

### Experimental Framework

**Uniform training protocol across all 12 architectures:**
- Training data: ASVspoof 2019 LA `train` + `dev` splits
- Loss: Cross-entropy with log-softmax
- Optimiser: Adam, lr = 0.0001 with learning rate scheduler
- Epochs: 100 with early stopping (patience = 5 epochs)
- Each experiment repeated **3 times with random initialisation**; mean ± std reported

**Two evaluation datasets:**
1. **ASVspoof 2019 LA `eval`**: In-domain, unseen attacks (A07–A19)
2. **In-The-Wild dataset (new)**: Out-of-domain, real-world deepfakes

**Three spectral feature types (513-dimensional each):**
- `cqtspec`: Constant-Q transform spectrogram — logarithmically-spaced frequency bins, better suited to pitch-based artefacts
- `logspec`: Log-scaled magnitude spectrogram
- `melspec`: Mel-scaled spectrogram — perceptually weighted, most widely used in speech processing

**Two audio input length modes:**
- `Full`: Variable-length input; global averaging layer added to architectures that require fixed-length input
- `4s`: Fixed-length 4-second clips; truncated (random subsegment) if longer, repeated if shorter

**End-to-end models** (CRNNSpoof, RawNet2, RawPC, RawGAT-ST) use raw waveforms directly.

### The In-The-Wild Dataset

A new evaluation dataset constructed from publicly available social media and video sharing content:

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Total duration | 37.9 hours |
| Fake audio | 17.2 hours |
| Genuine audio | 20.7 hours |
| Speakers | 58 celebrities and politicians |
| Avg clip length | 4.3 seconds |
| Sample rate | 16 kHz |
| Source | Social media, YouTube, podcasts, speeches |

**Fake clips**: Sourced from 219 publicly available files explicitly advertising audio deepfakes. The "out-of-character" nature of the clips (e.g., "Donald Trump reads Star Wars") makes ground-truth verification straightforward.

**Genuine clips**: Manually matched to fake clips by speaker, speaking style, emotional register, background noise level, and duration — controlled for confounds.

### Finding 1 — Fixed vs. Variable Input Length (Table 2)

| Input Mode | ASVspoof EER % | t-DCF | In-the-Wild EER % |
|-----------|--------------|-------|-----------------|
| **Full (variable)** | **9.85** | **0.22** | **60.10** |

**Using the full audio file halves ASVspoof EER** (9.85% vs 18.89%) and improves in-the-wild performance. Fixed-length truncation is a significant, unnecessary sacrifice of performance. Papers citing results on fixed-length inputs are reporting substantially degraded numbers.

### Finding 2 — Feature Extraction (Spectral Feature Type)

| Feature | Average Effect |
|---------|---------------|
| **cqtspec** | Best for spectral models — lowest EER on ASVspoof |
| **logspec** | Close second to cqtspec; sometimes best on in-the-wild |
| **melspec** | Worst — consistently outperformed by both alternatives |

**Replacing melspec with cqtspec improves average performance by 37% EER**, all other factors constant. This holds uniformly across all 8 spectral-feature architectures.

- LCNN melspec Full: 15.09% EER → LCNN cqtspec Full: 6.35% EER (2.4× improvement just from feature change)
- ResNet18 melspec Full: 21.66% EER → ResNet18 cqtspec Full: 6.55% EER (3.3× improvement)

### Finding 3 — Raw Waveform vs. Spectral Models

| Model Family | Best ASVspoof EER % | Best In-the-Wild EER % |
|-------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Raw waveform** | **1.2%** (RawGAT-ST) | **33.9%** (RawNet2) |
| Spectral (cqtspec/logspec) | ~6% (LCNN) | ~37% (MesoInception) |
| Spectral (melspec) | ~9% (LCNN-LSTM) | ~50%+ |

Raw waveform models outperform spectral models on ASVspoof. However, the in-the-wild advantage shrinks — the best spectral model (MesoInception logspec: 37.4%) is competitive with the best raw model (RawNet2: 33.9%). The assumption that raw waveform superiority on ASVspoof translates to real-world superiority is not confirmed.

### Finding 4 — Adding More ASVspoof Data Does Not Help

Training on all three ASVspoof 2019 splits (train + dev + **+ eval**, adding eval attacks A07–A19 as training data) for the best in-the-wild model (RawNet2 4s):
- With train+dev only: 33.9% in-the-wild EER
- With train+dev+eval: **33.1 ± 0.2% in-the-wild EER** — essentially no improvement

This confirms that the performance gap is **not a data quantity problem** — it is a fundamental domain mismatch. All ASVspoof splits are based on VCTK; adding more VCTK-derived attacks does not provide information applicable to real-world generalization.

## 6. Limitations and Weaknesses

1. **Shallow hyperparameter search**: The authors explicitly note that their implementations achieve 2–4% higher EER and 0.1 higher t-DCF than reported in original papers due to not fine-tuning hyperparameters. This means the controlled comparison undershoots each model's true potential — the ablations are informative but absolute numbers shouldn't be cited as representative of model capability

2. **In-the-wild dataset is English-only**: All 58 celebrities/politicians are English speakers. Cross-language generalization is not evaluated. Deepfakes of politicians in other languages (Zelensky example cited) represent a real threat not covered

3. **In-the-wild dataset construction bias**: Fake clips are sourced from content that explicitly advertises itself as a deepfake ("Donald Trump reads Star Wars"). These may not represent high-quality adversarial deepfakes designed to deceive. The ground truth is easy to verify, but the clips may be lower quality than targeted political misinformation deepfakes

4. **In-the-wild dataset size**: 37.9 hours is modest. EER standard deviations on this dataset are large (up to ±15%) across 3 seeds, indicating high variance. Conclusions about in-the-wild performance are statistically noisy

5. **No analysis of why generalisation fails**: The paper identifies that models fail on in-the-wild data but does not explain what artefacts they are learning (and failing to generalise). The companion paper (2106.12914) suggests silence patterns may be one factor, but this is not investigated here

6. **No domain adaptation experiments**: Having identified the generalisation problem, the paper does not explore potential fixes — domain adaptation, domain adversarial training, diverse training sources, or augmentation strategies. It diagnoses the problem without proposing a cure

7. **Fixed 513-dimensional features**: All spectral features use 513 dimensions, regardless of architecture. Different resolutions might be optimal for different architectures — the feature resolution is not ablated

8. **No analysis of which TTS algorithms cause the failure**: In-the-wild deepfakes are likely created with modern neural TTS (ElevenLabs, VALL-E, etc.) not present in ASVspoof. The paper doesn't characterise which generation technology produces the hardest-to-detect clips

9. **No AASIST or SSL-based models**: The paper's selection of 12 architectures predates AASIST and SSL-based countermeasures. These more recent, higher-performing models are not included in the in-the-wild evaluation

## 7. Key Takeaways

1. **ASVspoof benchmark performance does not predict real-world performance**: The single most important finding. A model achieving 1.2% EER on ASVspoof degrades to 37% EER on in-the-wild data — a roughly 30× degradation. Published ASVspoof numbers are not evidence of deployment readiness

2. **cqtspec consistently outperforms melspec by ~37%**: Across all architectures and conditions, the constant-Q transform feature is uniformly better than mel-scale for deepfake detection. This is an actionable engineering finding requiring no architectural change — just replacing one feature extractor

3. **Use full-length audio, not fixed-length truncation**: Full-length input halves ASVspoof EER (9.85% vs 18.89%) and improves in-the-wild performance. The common practice of truncating to 4 seconds unnecessarily sacrifices 50% of performance. Variable-length processing via global average pooling is the correct approach

4. **Raw waveform models dominate on ASVspoof but the advantage is smaller in-the-wild**: RawGAT-ST achieves 1.2% ASVspoof EER but 37% in-the-wild EER. A spectral model (MesoInception logspec) achieves 10% ASVspoof EER but 37% in-the-wild EER — essentially the same real-world capability despite a 10× gap on the benchmark

5. **More ASVspoof data doesn't fix the generalisation problem**: Training on all three splits of ASVspoof 2019 produces the same in-the-wild EER as training on just train+dev. The problem is distributional — VCTK-based data cannot teach models about diverse real-world speakers, microphones, and TTS systems

6. **Many reported performance differences are method artefacts, not genuine capability differences**: A model using melspec and 4s input versus one using cqtspec and full input can show a 37% + 50% combined difference in EER — purely from preprocessing choices that have nothing to do with the model architecture. This inflates apparent differences between architectures in the literature

7. **The field may have overfit to ASVspoof**: The combination of a single benchmark, studio-quality speech, and a fixed set of known TTS algorithms may have led the community to learn dataset-specific patterns. In-the-wild data breaks these patterns entirely

- Don't imply the In-the-Wild dataset is representative of all real-world deepfakes — it is English-only, celebrity-focused, and sourced from explicitly-labelled deepfake content
